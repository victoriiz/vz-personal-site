Built a Physics-Informed Neural Network (PINN) in PyTorch to solve the 2D incompressible Navier-Stokes equations for lid-driven cavity flow. The network learns fluid velocity and pressure fields by minimising PDE residuals computed via automatic differentiation — no simulation data required. Implemented SIREN (sinusoidal activation) architecture for stable higher-order derivative computation through the network.

Feb 2026 ★★★★★

One Day, Everyone Will Have Always Been Against This — Omar El Akkad

i hesitated to rate this book—to even go on reading past a certain point—because of how little connection I felt. yes, I could nod in agreement, let a few tears escape out of sympathy, bring a hand up to my face in horror, but I couldn't get rid of the awful sense of superficiality it all felt to me. it is discomfort, to read so plainly, and in so much unfettered detail the reality that is so completely far from the coziness of my life. It is this discomfort that Akkad lays so clearly. it is this ignorance, when, spread as a blanket over the Western sphere and more, becomes permission for the worst of humanity.

still, you have to keep reading. and by the end, you too may have that pessimistic, pragmatic hope that one day this reality would at the very least be condemned by everyone, that there is no greed or pointing of fingers. until then, we keep wringing our hands and hold fast to these individual courages.

"There's always room on a liberal's lawn."
March 2026 ★★★★☆

Cold Enough for Snow — Jessica Au

wow. for such a short novel, it sure did take me a long time to finish. or really, it was because I read it in bits and pieces, reading on bus rides, during passing periods between classes, or on weekend mornings when I felt too lazy to get out of bed. I think I am glad for this. this is not a book meant to be rushed through in a sitting. it is meant to seep and trickle and slowly ooze.

the novel carried a quiet warmth that exuded in everything. and there was a constant distance, too, in the tokyo rains or the passing memories or in the vagueness of the unnamed daughter and mother's relationship to the very end. though seemingly formatted as a documentation of the pair's travels, at times we are transported into the daughter's recollections and emotions or the rushing flow of a nostalgic anecdote.

though none of these are fully described dramatically, we cannot help but feel its twinge regardless. descriptions are offered instead to the simple things: objects, art, a lake, and so many more. in this way, this story too is normalcy wrapped in a blanket and presented to the reader, asking, here. take this. this is my passing gift to you, and oh, won't you come inside and stay awhile?

there is a mystery that remains after the last page. but it'll just be that. here is a quiet, unassuming yet introspective tale that has passed through my life at this moment in time. it'll just be that.

Dec 2025 ★★★★★

I Who Have Never Known Men — Jacqueline Harpman

**spoiler alert** it is without a doubt due to the curious, almost detached storytelling of the narrator that this book read so peacefully, even when describing tragedy. However, oblivious as the narrator may have been in the first half of the book, she did not lack sympathy—and it was endlessly interesting to me to watch her journey tying these knots of her understanding together, untying them again, and untangling them once more in her relentless search to learn to the very end.

on the other hand, it was this interest alone that drove me to continue turning the page. just as the plains were unchanging, there was rarely any excitement or thrill in the story. Still, I waited in anticipation to see if there would be that moment where the narrator Figures It Out, meets an ally, or simply escapes (but where to?).

as it turns out, she never truly does Figure It Out. She never does meet a man, much less another human being. And perhaps at the end, she really did find her own escape, but as the premise of the whole setting goes, she had simply traded her prison for a luxurious one. This plot, as a whole, is not fascinating in itself. It's not one that I expect to think about endlessly.

But I will think about it sometime, maybe, some boring day in the future when I am contemplating loneliness, or even taking this idea of living—of people—for granted. As the narrator hoped in her last days, her story did reach someone. Her story, which was so laced with quiet despair, yet unwavering hope—these feelings at least, will not leave.
